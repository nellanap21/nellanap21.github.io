A residential purchase agreement is a legally binding contract between a buyer and a seller outlining the terms and conditions for the sale of a residential property.

If you're planning to buy a home, you're going to need to sign a residential purchase agreement. The good thing is that the seller's real estate agent can provide the standard residential purchase agreement, but you still need to understand the key terms.

Chances are you don't have time to become a contract law expert and parse through the legalese. As a former lawyer who has bought 4 houses, I can help you understand how the residential purchase agreement works.

1. Purchase price

Purchase Price is the total amount of money agreed upon for the sale of the property. This includes the Loan Amount, which is the amount you plan to finance with a mortgage, and the Down Payment, cash you wire on the day of closing.

2. Close of escrow

ðŸ’¡ What is an escrow?

An escrow is an account controlled by a trusted third party, the escrow agent, which holds the buyer's money (your initial deposit) and transfers it to the buyer on the day of closing.

Initial Deposit (aka Earnest Money) is the amount of money you must transfer to the escrow agent within 3 days of an accepted offer. This is called "opening the escrow account." The deposit counts towards your down payment and is a signal that you are a serious buyer.

Close of escrow is the date you officially become the owner of the property. What happens is that the escrow agent transfers the money in the escrow account to the seller and officially closes the account. Typically this is 30 days after your offer is accepted.

Liquidated Damages means that if you break the contract, the seller will keep your initial deposit. ðŸ˜±

3. Contingencies

ðŸ’¡ What is a contingency?

A contingency is a requirement that must be satisfied before the sale can be completed. If a contingency is not met, the buyer can cancel the contract without penalty.

Contingencies are a buyer's best friend. They give you a right to terminate the contract for a limited number legitimate reasons. We'll get into the specific reasons below.

First, this is how the contingency process helps buyers.

  1. The contract lists a deadline for you to complete the contingency requirement
  2. After the contract is signed, you have to try in good faith to satisfy the requirement
  3. If you can satisfy the contingency requirement, you would remove the contingency, which means you have removed your cancellation right
  4. But, if you can't meet the contingency, you have until the deadline to "exercise" the contingency and cancel the contract

4. Loan contingency

Loan contingency makes your obligation to purchase the property dependent on obtaining mortgage financing. This protects you by allowing you to back out of a deal if you are unable to secure a loan.

ðŸ’¡ Home 101 Tip

17 days is the default deadline for you to get your loan approved by the bank. You would work with the bank to make sure they have everything they need to approve the loan.

5. Appraisal contingency

Appraisal contingency makes your obligation to purchase the property dependent on the property being appraised at a value equal to or higher than the agreed-upon purchase price. This protects you and the mortgage lender by ensuring that you are not overpaying for the property.

ðŸ’¡ Home 101 Tip

17 days is the default deadline for you to get your appraisal contigency. You don't have to do much here because typically your lender will order an appraisal report from a licensed appraiser.

6. Investigation of Property

Investigation of property is a contingency that allows you to conduct various inspections of the property before finalizing the purchase. This protects you by giving you an opportunity to identify any potential isssues with the property that may influence your decision to proceed with the purchase.

You probably aren't an expert in home inspections, so you can arrange for experts to do inspections, such as:

  • General home inspection
  • Pest inspection
  • Roof inspection
  • Plumbing, electrical, HVAC
  • Mold or radon
  • Foundation or structural engineering inspection
  • Sewer line or sepctic inspection
ðŸ’¡ Home 101 Tip

17 days is the default deadline for you to get inspection reports, get quotes for repairs, and decide whether to request repairs or request a lower purchase price. It's a lot to do by the deadline, so I recommend ordering your inspections as soon as possible after you have a signed contract.

7. Review of seller documents

Review of seller documents allows you to review various disclosures by the seller beofre committing to the purchase. This gives you the opportunity to ensure all information is disclosed and ask any follow-up questions about the property.

Here are some common disclosures to look for:

  • Seller property questionnaire
  • Agentâ€™s inspection disclosure
  • Reports on past inspections or repairs
ðŸ’¡ Home 101 Tip

In many states, sellers are actually required to complete a Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS). The TDS discloses known defects or issues with the property that may impact its value, including structural, electrical, and plumbing issues.

8. Preliminary title report

Preliminary title report is a contingency that allows you to review the title report before finalizing the purchase. This is something the seller's agent, escrow agent, or title company will provide.

Here are some issues to watch for when reviewing a preliminary title report.

  • Is the seller the current owner? Are there any disputes over the current ownership?
  • Are there any existing debts against the property that the buyer would inherit?
  • Are there any easements on the property that allow others to use the property?

9. Items included and excluded

Items included and excluded lays out exactly what is and is not part of the purchase. Here are items typically included:

  • Fixtures that are attached and not easily removed (light fixtures and built-ins)
  • Major appliances (refrigerator and waster/dryer)
  • Window treatments (shades and shutters)
  • Home systems (HVAC and water heaters)

These are items usually excluded:

  • Personal property (furniture)
  • Leased items like solar panels (unless the lease is transferred)
ðŸ’¡ Home 101 Tip

We've listed the standard, but you can always negotiate for something different. The buyer might want to keep a special hot tub, portable shed or chandelier.

10. Allocation of costs

Allocation of costs clarifies who will be responsible for paying various expenses associate with the transaction. Here are the costs typically paid by the seller:

  • Legally required Natural Hazard Zone Disclosure
  • Legally required smoke alarms, CO detectors and water heater bracing
  • Homeowners association transfer fees
  • County transfer taxes

These are costs typically paid by the buyer:

  • Escrow fees, although these can be split with the seller
  • Owner's title insurance policy (this protects you)
  • Lender's title insurance policy (this protects your lender)
  • Home warranty plan but seller can offer to pay as an incentive
