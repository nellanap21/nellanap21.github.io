How to handle appraisal contingency

The appraisal contingency covers to the actual value of the property you want to buy. If the property appraises for less than the purchase price, then bank won’t give you the full amount of the mortgage and you won’t be obligated to close.

Here is how I handled the appraisal contingency.

  1. Check that bank ordered an appraisal
  2. Get the actual appraisal report
  3. Review the report

Let's take it from the top

1. check that bank ordered an appraisal

This is an example email that you can send. Our contract was fully signed on October 11 and the follow-up email was sent on October 19.

If you haven’t heard anything about an appraisal one week after signing, send a follow-up email

2. get the actual appraisal report

In our case, the bank had already ordered the appraisal and got the report back.

3. review the report

This is our appraisal report. The only number you care about is the final valuation.

Page 1 describes the property, the contract, the neighborhood, site, and home improvements.

Page 2 describes how the appraiser did an analysis of comparable home sales to arrive at a market valuation of $735,000. The highlight section is the most important part of the appraisal report.

Scenario 1: Valuation comes in at or above price

If the valuation came in at or above the purchase price, then everything is good and there is nothing else for you to do. You can now remove the appraisal contingency by filling out a contingency removal form.

This is an example of how you would fill out a contingency removal form.

Scenario 2: Valuation comes in below price

If the appraised value is less than the purchase price, you can either (1) make up the difference in cash, (2) cancel the deal, or (3) get the seller to lower the price to the appraised value.

This is an example how you would fill out the cancellation form.
