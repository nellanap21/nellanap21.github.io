üí° What is an inspection contingency?

The inspection contingency (aka investigation of property) is a clause in the residential purchase agreement that allows you to conduct various inspections of the property berfore finalizing the purchase. This protects you by giving you the opportunity to find any serious problems with the property that may cause you to change your mind about buying the house.

‍

It's true that inspection rights haven't always been the easiest or fastest thing to do. But it doesn't have to be a dreaded task. With a few tips and the right process, you can take the inspection process from a necessary evil to a big money-saver.

That's great news since lots of people rely on inspection rights to get a better understanding of the home they are about to buy. Without it, you can expect hidden defects and other unknown problems with your purchase.

With a little planning (and a helpful guide üòâ), you can identify all the repairs you will need to make to the house and get paid to make those repairs.

1. Schedule inspections

After signing the contract, you should schedule inspections as soon as possible because you agreed to a deadline for completing your inspections.

The purchase contract you signed allows you to inspect any physical attributes of the property, and at a minimum you should schedule a home inspection, roof inspection, and pest inspection.

If you want to be extra thorough, you can also order a sewer, chimney, foundation, HVAC, and electrical inspection.

You bear the cost of the inspection, but I think it‚Äôs always worth the cost. This is the biggest purchase of your life and you need to know if there are any hidden dealbreakers.

2. Attend the Inspections

Although the inspector will typically give you a written report, you should be at the property in person so you can talk to the inspector and ask questions.

When we first started house hunting in the SF Bay Area, we found this house on a mountain with amazing views. We fell in love with the property, made a bid, and got a signed contract.

We hired a foundation inspector and while we walked the property, he pulled me over and told me about the white pipes sticking out of the hill. I noticed them before but thought nothing of it. It turns out those pipes were installed for drainage after a previous landslide!

3. Review the reports

You should read your inspection reports but please note that inspectors are usually very thorough. It‚Äôs their job to find as many problems as possible.

4. Decide how to address issues

Option A: cancel the contract

Let‚Äôs go back to the example above where I found out about the landslide repair in the back yard. This made me worried about the foundation of the house.

But it‚Äôs mentally very difficult to change your mind on a house that you bid on, won the contract for, and started thinking about as your home.

However, with storms becoming increasingly severe, I didn‚Äôt want to be worried about landslides every time we had a big storm.

After talking with family, we decided to cancel the contract. See below for how to fill out a form to cancel a contract.

Check the paragraph of the purchase contract that allows you to cancel. In this case, Section 8C refers to the ‚ÄúInvestigation of Property‚Äù contingency.

Add the title company that serves as your escrow agent. A full release means that you will get your full deposit back.

Both the buyer and seller sign the cancellation form.

Option B: request repairs

If you find any hidden defects with the property that you weren‚Äôt aware of when making the bid, you can ask that the seller make repairs.

However, I don‚Äôt recommend this approach because the seller is not incentivized to do a great job on the repairs.

It‚Äôs better to take option C to get money from the seller so you can do repairs yourself after closing.

Option C: request concessions

If you find hidden defects with the property, you can ask that the seller cover the cost of repairs.

As an example, in our home inspection, we found a number of hidden defects that showed the house was in much worse shape than a reasonable person would expect.

Here are some things that we discovered. For example, we could not have known that the pipes in the crawl space were rusted out or that the 3 prong receptacles were not properly grounded.

Tip

One of the inspectors actually told our agent that this contractor has a reputation for doing some shoddy cosmetic work right before a sale. It‚Äôs really helpful to chat with the inspector.

The sewer drain pipe was about to break from rust. Inspector said if he scraped the pipe with a screw driver, it would have created a hole.

Some electrical receptacles looked to be 3 hole receptacles but they were not properly grounded. They were just 2 prong receptacles with a third hole. Some receptacles were wired backwards. This is a sign of a superficial renovation.

Some of the air ducts were totally disconnected. Basically, we were heating the crawl space more than the house.

Tip

It‚Äôs really important that you schedule the inspections as soon as possible because you need to leave time to get quotes for any repairs. With quotes in hand, you are signaling that you legitimately need to and will make these repairs after closing.
