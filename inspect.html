How to handle inspection contingency

  1. Schedule the Inspections
  2. Attend the Inspections
  3. Review the reports
  4. Decide how to address issues
  5. Send Email to Sellers
  6. Review Seller Response

Let's take it from the top

1. Schedule the Inspections

After signing the contract, you should schedule inspections as soon as possible because you agreed to a deadline for completing your inspections.

The purchase contract you signed allows you to inspect any physical attributes of the property, and at a minimum you should schedule a home inspection, roof inspection, and pest inspection.

If you want to be extra thorough, you can also order a sewer, chimney, foundation, HVAC, and electrical inspection.

You bear the cost of the inspection, but I think it’s always worth the cost. This is the biggest purchase of your life and you need to know if there are any hidden dealbreakers.

2. Attend the Inspections

Although the inspector will typically give you a written report, you should be at the property in person so you can talk to the inspector and ask questions.

When we first started house hunting in the SF Bay Area, we found this house on a mountain with amazing views. We fell in love with the property, made a bid, and got a signed contract.

We hired a foundation inspector and while we walked the property, he pulled me over and told me about the white pipes sticking out of the hill. I noticed them before but thought nothing of it. It turns out those pipes were installed for drainage after a previous landslide!

3. Review the reports

You should read your inspection reports but please note that inspectors are usually very thorough. It’s their job to find as many problems as possible.

Our home inspector recommended that the red “Action Items” are the things we should take care of immediately while blue “Consideration Items” are things that you can take care of later.

4. Decide how to address issues

Option A: cancel the contract

Let’s go back to the example above where I found out about the landslide repair in the back yard. This made me worried about the foundation of the house.

But it’s mentally very difficult to change your mind on a house that you bid on, won the contract for, and started thinking about as your home.

However, with storms becoming increasingly severe, I didn’t want to be worried about landslides every time we had a big storm.

After talking with family, we decided to cancel the contract. See below for how to fill out a form to cancel a contract.

Check the paragraph of the purchase contract that allows you to cancel. In this case, Section 8C refers to the “Investigation of Property” contingency.

Add the title company that serves as your escrow agent. A full release means that you will get your full deposit back.

Both the buyer and seller sign the cancellation form.

Option B: request repairs

If you find any hidden defects with the property that you weren’t aware of when making the bid, you can ask that the seller make repairs.

However, I don’t recommend this approach because the seller is not incentivized to do a great job on the repairs.

It’s better to take option C to get money from the seller so you can do repairs yourself after closing.

Option C: request concessions

If you find hidden defects with the property, you can ask that the seller cover the cost of repairs.

As an example, in our home inspection, we found a number of hidden defects that showed the house was in much worse shape than a reasonable person would expect.

Here are some things that we discovered. For example, we could not have known that the pipes in the crawl space were rusted out or that the 3 prong receptacles were not properly grounded.

Tip

One of the inspectors actually told our agent that this contractor has a reputation for doing some shoddy cosmetic work right before a sale. It’s really helpful to chat with the inspector.

The sewer drain pipe was about to break from rust. Inspector said if he scraped the pipe with a screw driver, it would have created a hole.

Some electrical receptacles looked to be 3 hole receptacles but they were not properly grounded. They were just 2 prong receptacles with a third hole. Some receptacles were wired backwards. This is a sign of a superficial renovation.

Some of the air ducts were totally disconnected. Basically, we were heating the crawl space more than the house.

Tip

It’s really important that you schedule the inspections as soon as possible because you need to leave time to get quotes for any repairs. With quotes in hand, you are signaling that you legitimately need to and will make these repairs after closing.

After reviewing the inspection reports and getting quotes for the repairs, this is the summary email we sent regarding the request for concessions.

Our original purchase agreement was for $735,000. We then requested a concession of approximately $60,000 to cover the repairs needed for the house. The seller agreed to concession of about $40,000. You have to get this amendment in writing on the proper form.

We papered the concession by signing an addendum to the purchase agreement. The seller did not have to come out of pocket for the costs because we just reduced the purchase price (by about $25,000) and had seller provide a credit towards closing costs (by about $16,450)
