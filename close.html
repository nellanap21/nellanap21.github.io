How to handle closing

After you have removed all of the contingencies in the real estate purchase contract, you are ready to hold the closing of escrow (aka closing), which is the day you officially become the owner of the property.

At this point, you’ll be working with the Loan Closer at the bank and the Escrow Agent at the title company to make sure the final numbers are correct before wiring the funds and signing the docs. Here are the steps I took to get to closing.

  1. Set a target date for closing
  2. Ask for the pre-closing statement
  3. Check the pre-closing statement
  4. Fix any mistakes
  5. Double check the pre-closing statement
  6. Wire the funds and sign the docs
  7. Sign the documents
  8. Closing

1. Set a target date for closing

Set a target date for closing. You don’t have to close on that exact target date, but it’s helpful to have a deadline for everyone to work towards.

2. Ask for the pre-closing statement

The pre-closing statement is the final set of calculations for the purchase of the property.

I started an email chain with Roshon (title company) and Michael (bank) and asked for the pre-closing statement.

3. Check the pre-closing statement

On November 6, I received the estimated pre-closing statement (also known as the Master Statement or Closing Disclosure). This is where you should check that the numbers are correct.

4. Fix any mistakes

The loan coordinator found a mistake in the pre-closing statement.

The contract had a seller credit of $16k to pay for our closing costs, but our closing costs were only $13k. As a result, we could only have a $13k seller credit to offset our closing costs. To cover the remainder, we lowered the purchase price by $3k.

The next day, we had to sign an additional addendum to paper the change to seller credit and purchase price.

5. Double check the pre-closing statement

Once you get the final pre-closing statement (also called the final buyer’s statement), double check the numbers.

The Financial Consideration is made up of a deposit, loan, down payment, and seller credit

County Taxes are pro-rated. The seller paid property taxes for the second half of 2023 so I had to reimburse the seller for property taxes paid from the date of closing to the rest of the year.

Loan Charges are the closing costs you need to pay the bank.

Title Charges are the closing costs you need to pay the title company.

Government Charges are government filing fees.

Homeowners Insurance for the first year can be paid as part of closing. The bank will collect your premium as part of closing costs and pay the insurance company.

6. Wire the funds

Once you have the final closing statement, you know how much money you need to wire to the escrow agent in order to close.

Go to your bank and wire the funds to the escrow agent. The escrow agent will give you the wire instructions. Be really careful here!

Once you send the wire, email the escrow agent to confirm receipt.

7. Sign the documents

Now you need to sign the documents in front of a notary who will verify your identity.

The escrow agent can send a notary to you to do the signing, but you can also offer do the signing at the escrow agent’s office. Doing it in person saved me the $200 fee for sending a notary to me.

8. Closing

The escrow agent then collects your down payment wire, combines it with the bank’s wire of funds, confirms that everything matches the final closing statement, and sends the money to the seller.

This is an example confirmation email that you have officially closed!
